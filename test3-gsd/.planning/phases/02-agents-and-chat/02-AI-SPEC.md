## 1. System Classification

**System Type:** Multi-Agent (hierarchical: coordinator + research sub-agent + compilation sub-agent)

**Description:**
A coordinator agent runs in a local single-user chat UI (assistant-ui on a Hono SSE backend) and orchestrates two sub-agents: a research sub-agent that searches the web via Tavily and lands findings as `sources` and `claims` rows in OneBrain (Postgres + pgvector), and a compilation sub-agent that holds the only `vault_write_atomic` capability and updates the Obsidian vault on user-triggered recompile. "Good" means: every quantitative claim traces back to a real source row written before the claim; the coordinator pushes back substantively (not rhetorically) when the user asserts unsourced quantitative claims; the research sub-agent emits strict-JSON output that conforms to a Zod schema; the compilation sub-agent is provably the sole writer to the vault; and the wiki view stays a derivative of OneBrain rather than diverging from it.

**Critical Failure Modes:**
The 3-5 behaviors that absolutely cannot go wrong in this system:
1. **Wrong-agent vault write** — any agent other than `compilation` writing into `vault/` (violates Pitfall 5 / COMP-10 / the project's load-bearing single-writer commitment).
2. **Quantitative-claim hallucination** — a TAM-shaped or ≥$1M numeric claim landing without a `cites_source` edge to a real source row (AGENT-08 / Pitfall 19).
3. **Sub-agent prose smuggling** — coordinator quoting the research sub-agent's free-text `summary` verbatim into chat instead of re-fetching live claim rows from OneBrain (Pitfall 18 / D-06).
4. **Pushback theater** — coordinator producing rhetorical "be careful with that number" pushback that does not state the rule, the action taken, or the path forward (CRIT-01 / Pitfall 16 / D-07).
5. **Schema-malformed sub-agent output** — research sub-agent returning JSON that fails Zod validation and the coordinator continuing as if it succeeded (D-04).

## 1b. Domain Context

> Researched by `gsd-domain-researcher`. Grounds the evaluation strategy in domain expert knowledge.

**Industry Vertical:** Strategic business planning / investor-readiness / venture-backed go-to-market planning. Practitioner community spans founders building toward a Series A raise, strategy consultants (MBB / Big-4), in-house corporate-strategy teams, and the VC investment-committee readers who consume the artifacts these tools produce.

**User Population:** A single founder/operator (this user) using the system as a critical thinking partner while drafting strategic-framework pages (SWOT, STP, 4Ps, Porter's 5 Forces, ICP, persona, positioning, JTBD) that will eventually feed an investor-grade pitch. The downstream "judge" is not the user — it is a Series-A investment committee reading a memo or pitch deck whose claims trace back through this wiki to OneBrain rows.

**Stakes Level:** **High.** This is a personal tool with no production deployment, but the artifacts it produces (claims, framework pages, eventually a pitch deck) are intended to face investors. A confidently-stated fabricated TAM number, a hand-waved Porter's analysis, or a CAC/LTV ratio asserted without a comp set can each be a meeting-ending credibility failure in front of an IC. The tool itself has no regulatory exposure; the artifacts it produces carry the founder's reputational and fundraising risk.

**Output Consequence:** Coordinator chat replies and the wiki pages they seed become the source material the founder uses to build pitch narrative, model assumptions, and IC-memo content. A claim that lands in OneBrain without a real source row, or a Porter's force filled in with generic-industry boilerplate, propagates from the wiki into the pitch and is what an investor will challenge in due diligence. Provenance must be reconstructible from any wiki claim back to a real, retrievable web source.

### What Domain Experts Evaluate Against

These are rubric ingredients in the language of the practitioner community (founders, IC readers, strategy consultants) — not AI-jargon. The downstream eval-planner should design Phase 2 agent-behavior tests against these.

```
Dimension: Quantitative-claim provenance ("show me the source")
Good: Every numeric claim above the noise floor (TAM/SAM/SOM, ARR target, CAC, LTV, payback period, market growth %, churn rate, unit price, competitor share) is rendered with a citation that resolves to a real, retrievable URL whose page text actually contains the number — not a misattributed paraphrase.
Bad: A confident number with no citation, OR a citation that resolves to a real source which does not contain the claim, OR a citation attributed to "McKinsey" that was actually BCG/Gartner/HBR (Stanford HAI: leading models hallucinate citations 58–82% of the time on specific factual lookups; CS-paper hallucinated citations rose 9× from 0.3% in 2024 to 2.6% in 2025).
Stakes: Critical
Source: AI-hallucinated-citations research (Stanford HAI; Development Corporate analysis of 18,000 CS papers); investor due-diligence convention that every quantitative claim in a pitch must be sourceable.
```

```
Dimension: Market-sizing rigor (TAM / SAM / SOM triangulation)
Good: TAM stated with the method shown (top-down anchored on a named industry-data source — IDC, Gartner, census, etc.); SAM derived bottom-up from a defensible filter (geography × segment × ACV × reachable accounts); SOM grounded in current capacity (sales-team math: reps × quota attainment × ACV). Top-down and bottom-up triangulate to within ~2× of each other; if they don't, the gap is acknowledged.
Bad: "Vanity TAM" — quoting the largest available industry number ($5T healthcare, $X trillion AI) with no narrowing. The "1% fallacy" — "we just need 1% of the market." Single-method sizing (top-down only, or bottom-up only) presented as multi-method. SOM that says $2M while the financial model says $5M (misalignment).
Stakes: Critical
Source: spectup, GoingVC, ForumVC, Antler practitioner writeups on market-sizing pitch failures. "TAM gets you the meeting; a rigorous bottom-up SOM gets you the term sheet."
```

```
Dimension: Unit-economics realism
Good: CAC stated with the cost components named (sales salary, marketing spend, tooling, allocated overhead — not just paid-channel CAC); LTV stated with the churn assumption visible; LTV:CAC ratio in the 3:1–5:1 healthy range OR explicitly framed as a target the business is not yet at; CAC payback period stated in months (Series-A expectation: <12 months; Series-B stretch: 12–15; >24 months is a red flag); contribution margin separated from gross margin.
Bad: Hand-waved CAC (paid-channel-only, sales/overhead excluded — typical 2–3× under-statement per Phoenix Strategy); LTV without a stated churn assumption; LTV:CAC < 3:1 presented as "healthy"; payback period missing or > 24 months without acknowledgment; "contribution margin" used interchangeably with "gross margin."
Stakes: Critical
Source: Phoenix Strategy Group, Kruze, Dodo Payments unit-economics guides; 2025–2026 VC convention that "Burn Multiple, LTV:CAC, NRR are the main indicators of founder competency."
```

```
Dimension: Competitive-positioning honesty (Porter's 5 Forces and adjacent)
Good: Each force filled in with named competitors, named substitutes, named buyer/supplier concentration figures; industry defined at line-of-business level (e.g., "cardiovascular diagnostic software" not "global healthcare"); biggest-competitor-is-the-status-quo (Excel, doing-nothing) acknowledged in SOM and Porter's "threat of substitutes."
Bad: Generic-industry boilerplate ("buyer power is moderate"); industry defined too broadly to be analytically useful; all five forces treated as equally important; competitors listed with no positioning differentiation; no named substitute or "do nothing" alternative.
Stakes: High
Source: HBS Institute for Strategy & Competitiveness, Mindtools, LinkedIn Advice on Five-Forces pitfalls. Porter's own guidance: "use at line-of-business level, not industry sector."
```

```
Dimension: ICP / persona specificity
Good: ICP defined firmographically (industry, company size band, revenue band, geography, tech stack, named-account examples) and behaviorally (job-to-be-done, current alternative, trigger event); persona has a named role, a buying-committee position, and a measurable pain — not just demographics.
Bad: ICP that reads as a market segment, not a customer ("SMBs in North America"); persona-as-fluff (name, age, hobbies, no buying behavior); no firmographic filters that map to a sales-prospecting list; JTBD stated as a feature wish, not a job.
Stakes: High
Source: JTBD practitioner literature (Christensen, Ulwick); standard B2B sales-ops convention that an ICP must map to a TAL (target-account list).
```

```
Dimension: Hypothesis discipline and contradiction handling
Good: Claims that are not yet evidenced are framed as hypotheses with explicit confidence (per CRIT-02/03); when two sources disagree (engineering says 12 weeks, sales promised 8), both views are preserved as a contradiction callout, not smoothed to a midpoint; the system does not auto-promote `hypothesis` to `validated` without an explicit evidence edge.
Bad: A speculative claim ("$99/mo will be acceptable to customers") presented as fact; contradicting source data silently averaged or dropped; high-confidence claims with thin supporting evidence (`confidence > 0.75 && supporting_count < 2` — the AGENT-05 trigger condition, deferred to Phase 4 but the rubric ingredient is here).
Stakes: High
Source: Nate B Jones hybrid-architecture transcript (contradiction = strategic signal, not noise); CRIT-02..06; PROJECT.md hypothesis-by-default identity.
```

```
Dimension: Pushback substance (CRIT-01 anchor)
Good: When the user asserts an unsourced quantitative claim, the system's response (a) names the rule that was violated ("that's a TAM-shaped number with no source"), (b) states the action it has taken ("I'm logging it as a hypothesis at confidence ~0.3"), and (c) names the path forward ("give me a source, or want me to research it?"). Direct, specific, not refusal.
Bad: Pushback theater — "be careful with that number" or "we should validate this" with no rule-naming, no action, no path forward. Or hard-veto refusal that blocks the user from logging the intent at all (also wrong — the user's assertion should still land as a hypothesis).
Stakes: Critical
Source: D-07; CLAUDE.md identity ("critical thinking partner, not refusal-bot"); Pitfall 16 ("pushback theater") in PITFALLS.md.
```

### Known Failure Modes in This Domain

These are domain-specific failure surfaces — how generic AI failure modes manifest in investor-prep work.

1. **TAM inflation by aggregation.** AI summarizes a Gartner press release and lifts the headline industry number into a TAM cell with no narrowing — produces a $50B TAM for what is actually a $200M wedge. The "vanity TAM" failure pattern from spectup / GoingVC. In this system: prevented at the schema layer by the AGENT-08 / Pitfall 19 ≥$1M / TAM-shaped guard, plus D-05 source-row-first ordering.

2. **Misattributed citations.** AI cites "according to McKinsey" when the underlying source was BCG, or fabricates a DOI/URL that 404s. Stanford HAI documented 58–82% citation-hallucination rates on specific factual lookups; the CS-papers study found 20% of AI-generated references fully fabricated and 45% with invalid DOIs. In this system: prevented by requiring `onebrain_write_source` (which captures the real URL + content + retrieval timestamp) *before* any `onebrain_write_claim` references it, and by the coordinator citing the live OneBrain `claim` row's `cites_source_ids[]` rather than re-paraphrasing the sub-agent's prose.

3. **"$1M ARR is achievable in 18 months" with no comp set.** Confident growth assertion with no anchoring in (a) comparable-company benchmarks, (b) sales-team math (reps × quota × ACV), or (c) current ARR baseline. This is the "unrealistic projections" failure pattern that VCs cite as a Series-A red flag. In this system: surfaced as a CRIT-01 pushback opportunity for the coordinator — name the missing comp set, log the assertion as hypothesis at low confidence, offer to research comparables.

4. **Porter's 5 Forces filled with generic-industry boilerplate.** "Threat of new entrants: moderate. Bargaining power of buyers: high." With no named buyers, no named entrants, no threshold of substitutability. The framework gets ticked off but produces no analytical signal. In this system: a Phase-5 STRAT-04 renderer concern primarily, but the Phase-2 research sub-agent must surface *named* competitors / substitutes / buyer concentration figures as discrete claims, not roll them into a synthesized "moderate."

5. **Devil's-advocate produces strawmen instead of substantive counter-claims.** A counter-claim must be grounded in a retrievable source — otherwise the system performs critical-thinking theater. (Phase 4 scope via AGENT-05; Phase 2's coordinator-pushback discipline is the precursor — pushback must cite the gap, not invent one.)

6. **Sub-agent prose smuggling.** Research sub-agent emits a `summary` field; coordinator quotes it verbatim to the user. The summary may have been written before the claim rows actually landed in OneBrain (or with subtle wording drift). The user reads the summary and trusts it as system output, but it has no provenance edge. In this system: D-06 hard rule — coordinator never quotes sub-agent prose; re-fetches `claim_ids_written` from OneBrain and cites the live row's `text` + `confidence`.

7. **Financial assumptions stated as facts.** "$99/mo is the right price point." (Hypothesis stated as fact.) The PROJECT.md identity demands `status = hypothesis` until evidence promotes; the failure mode is the assertion drifting upward in apparent confidence between chat → wiki → pitch deck without a corresponding evidence trail. Mitigation: D-09 hypothesis framing in chat (cite claim ID + confidence), CRIT-02 default-to-hypothesis on creation, no auto-promotion.

### Regulatory / Compliance Context

**No regulatory obligations attach to this system itself.** It is single-user, local-only, no data leaves the user's machine except via Tavily search (which the user already accepts) and Anthropic API calls (same).

**One material flag worth recording:** the artifacts this system produces (pitch decks, IC memos, financial assumptions) may be presented to investors. In the United States, materially misleading statements made to investors during a securities-law-regulated raise (e.g., a Reg D 506(b)/(c) round) can create liability for the founder under Rule 10b-5 and Section 17(a). The system has no SEC obligation; the *founder using the system* does. This is why the quantitative-claim hallucination guard (AGENT-08 / Pitfall 19) and the citation-provenance discipline (D-05, D-06) are framed as Critical-stakes — the cost of a hallucinated number isn't a failed eval, it's the founder unknowingly presenting fabricated data in a regulated raise. **Implication for evals:** the system should never produce a confident-sounding quantitative claim without a retrievable source. The bias should be toward "I logged this as a hypothesis at 0.3 confidence" rather than "here is the TAM."

GDPR / CCPA / HIPAA: no PII or PHI flows through the system in v1; out of scope.

## 2. Framework Decision

**Selected Framework:** `@anthropic-ai/claude-agent-sdk` (TypeScript), supplemented by `@anthropic-ai/sdk` for fine-grained model calls.

**Version:** `@anthropic-ai/claude-agent-sdk ~0.2.x` (exact pin at install — 0.x has churn per STACK.md). `@anthropic-ai/sdk ~0.90.x`.

**Rationale:**
This is a hierarchical multi-agent system on a hard-pinned Claude Opus model. The Agent SDK's primitives map 1:1 onto every Phase 2 decision: per-agent tool allowlists implement the single-writer-to-vault gate (`vault_write_atomic` exposed only to the compilation sub-agent — COMP-10 / Pitfall 5); `outputSchema` realizes D-04's strict-JSON contract with Zod; `settingSources` loads the coordinator's `CLAUDE.md` identity per the write/pushback/sub-agent-usage clauses; SDK hooks stream tool-call traces into UI-03 without a parallel logging layer; in-process MCP via `createSdkMcpServer` keeps the OneBrain tools (`onebrain_write_*`, `onebrain_search`, `vault_*`, `tavily_*`) co-located in TypeScript with the rest of the codebase. The SDK's "Claude vendor lock-in" critique is moot because Claude Opus is itself a project hard constraint.

**Alternatives Considered:**

| Framework | Ruled Out Because |
|-----------|------------------|
| LangChain / LangGraph | Explicitly out-of-scope per `.planning/REQUIREMENTS.md` ("Out of Scope") and `CLAUDE.md`. The user does not want chain/graph abstractions over what is already a small in-process agent topology. |
| Mastra (TS agent framework, 1.0 Jan 2026) | Credible TypeScript-native batteries-included alternative, but would forfeit Claude-Opus tuning, MCP depth, and per-agent tool gating that the Agent SDK provides natively. STACK.md already evaluated and rejected for v1. |
| OpenAI Agents SDK | Project is committed to Claude Opus; using OpenAI's SDK against Claude defeats its purpose. |
| CrewAI / AutoGen | Python-only (CrewAI) or fragmented ecosystem (AutoGen → AG2 → Microsoft Agent Framework); the project is Node 22 + TypeScript 5.6 and needs the SDK to compose with Hono / assistant-ui / Vercel AI SDK 6 in the same runtime. |
| Direct `@anthropic-ai/sdk` only (no Agent SDK) | Would force hand-rolling sub-agent spawning, tool-permission gating, structured-output retry, and hook-based tracing — exactly what the Agent SDK already implements. We still keep `@anthropic-ai/sdk` for any narrow direct call site, but the Agent SDK is the orchestration layer. |

**Vendor Lock-In Accepted:** Yes — Anthropic / Claude Opus. This is a conscious project-level commitment, not a Phase 2 decision; the project's hard architectural commitments (CLAUDE.md, PROJECT.md) name Claude Opus and the Agent SDK explicitly. Switching providers is an out-of-scope change.

### Entry Point Pattern

The coordinator is a `query()` invocation per chat turn. Sub-agents are declared in the `agents` map; each sub-agent's `tools` array is the authoritative allowlist for that role (this is the mechanism that enforces single-writer-to-vault — COMP-10 / Pitfall 5).

```ts
// src/agents/coordinator.ts
export async function* runCoordinatorTurn(userMessage: string) {
  const onebrainTools = createSdkMcpServer({
    name: 'onebrain',
    tools: [
      onebrain_write_source, onebrain_write_claim, onebrain_write_edge,
      onebrain_search,
    ],
  });
  const tavilyTools = createSdkMcpServer({ name: 'tavily', tools: [tavily_search, tavily_extract, tavily_crawl] });
  const vaultTools  = createSdkMcpServer({ name: 'vault',  tools: [vault_read, vault_write_atomic] });

  const result = query({
    prompt: userMessage,
    options: {
      model: 'claude-opus-4-7',                       // coordinator is reasoning-heavy
      settingSources: ['./CLAUDE.md'],                // identity / write-protocol / pushback templates
      mcpServers: { onebrain: onebrainTools, tavily: tavilyTools, vault: vaultTools },
      // Coordinator's own tool allowlist — note: NO vault_write_atomic.
      allowedTools: [
        'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_search',
        'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_write_source',  // coordinator may write a user-asserted claim/source directly
        'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_write_claim',
        'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_write_edge',
      ],
      agents: {
        research: {
          description: 'Search the web via Tavily and write findings to OneBrain. Stops at ~10 claims or ~120s.',
          prompt: researchPrompt,                     // D-04 strict-output contract spelled out here
          model: 'claude-sonnet-4-6',
          tools: [
            'mcp__tavily__tavily_search', 'mcp__tavily__tavily_extract', 'mcp__tavily__tavily_crawl',
            'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_search',
            'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_write_source', 'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_write_claim',
            'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_write_edge',
          ],
          // Strict JSON output validated against ResearchOutput schema (Zod) — see Section 4b.
        },
        compilation: {
          description: 'The ONLY agent allowed to write to vault/. Wraps runCompile().',
          prompt: compilationPrompt,
          model: 'claude-sonnet-4-6',
          tools: [
            'mcp__onebrain__onebrain_search',
            'mcp__vault__vault_read',
            'mcp__vault__vault_write_atomic',          // sole holder of the write capability
          ],
        },
      },
      hooks: {
        // Stream tool-call events out for the SSE → assistant-ui tool trace (UI-03)
        onToolCall: (e) => toolTraceSink.emit(e),
        onToolResult: (e) => toolTraceSink.emit(e),
      },
    },
  });

  for await (const ev of result) yield ev;             // forward to Hono SSE handler
}
```

### Key Abstractions

| Concept | What It Is | When You Use It |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| `query()` | Top-level coordinator invocation. Returns an async iterator of message/tool events. | One per chat turn (Phase 2) — driven by `POST /chat` on Hono. |
| `AgentDefinition` (the `agents: { ... }` map entries) | Declarative sub-agent: `{ description, prompt, model, tools[] }`. The `tools` array is the per-agent tool allowlist. | One entry per sub-agent: `research`, `compilation` in Phase 2; `ingest`, `financial`, `devils-advocate` add in Phase 4. |
| `tool()` | Wrap a TypeScript function as an MCP tool with a Zod input schema and a typed return. | Every OneBrain / vault / Tavily call site. Tools live in `src/agents/tools/`. |
| `createSdkMcpServer()` | Bundle a set of `tool()` definitions into a named MCP server that the SDK can attach to `query()` via `mcpServers`. | Three servers in Phase 2: `onebrain`, `tavily`, `vault`. The MCP namespace prefix (`mcp__<server>__<tool>`) is what appears in `allowedTools` / `tools`. |
| `settingSources` | List of files whose contents are loaded as part of the system prompt at boot. | Coordinator loads `./CLAUDE.md`. Sub-agents do **not** load CLAUDE.md — their `prompt` field is their full system prompt. |
| Sub-agent output schema (D-04) | A Zod schema the sub-agent's final message is parsed against. SDK retries once on parse failure; second failure surfaces a typed error. | The research sub-agent's `ResearchOutput` schema in `src/onebrain/types.ts`. The coordinator never accepts a sub-agent's free-text reply — only the parsed object. |
| Hooks (`onToolCall`, `onToolResult`, etc.) | SDK lifecycle callbacks. Fired on every tool invocation regardless of which agent invoked it. | Tool-trace fanout to UI-03; also where any belt-and-braces logging lives (e.g., assert at `onToolCall` that no non-compilation agent invoked `vault_write_atomic`). |

### Common Pitfalls

1. **0.x version churn — pin exact, don't use a caret.** Re-verify import paths against the installed SDK before relying on examples; minor releases between Phase 2 plan and Phase 2 execute can rename or restructure exports. The v2 interface preview is explicitly unstable per STACK.md.
2. **MCP tool naming in `allowedTools`/`tools`.** The full ID is `mcp__<server-name>__<tool-name>`. Forgetting the prefix or mismatching the server name silently disables the tool — the agent gets no error, the tool just isn't visible to it. Compilation cannot write to the vault if its `tools` list says `vault_write_atomic` instead of `mcp__vault__vault_write_atomic`. This is the most likely way to *accidentally* break the single-writer gate.
3. **Coordinator inheriting sub-agent tools.** The coordinator's `allowedTools` and each sub-agent's `tools` are independent allowlists. Putting `vault_write_atomic` on the coordinator's list "to be safe" defeats the gate. Phase 2 must lock-in: the coordinator's allowlist contains zero `vault__*` tools.
4. **Sub-agent prose smuggling (Pitfall 18).** Sub-agents return both a parsed object (when `outputSchema` matches) and a streamed text channel. The coordinator MUST consume only the parsed object; the streamed text is for trace/debug only. Re-fetch each `claim_ids_written` from OneBrain before quoting in chat (D-06).
5. **Sub-agents cannot spawn sub-agents.** Per the SDK design, only the top-level `query()` can declare sub-agents. The compilation sub-agent cannot invoke a "lint" sub-agent inside itself in Phase 2; that's a Phase 3+ pattern that has to live at the coordinator level.
6. **`maxSubAgents` and budget caps.** Set explicit caps (the SDK exposes a max-sub-agent-invocations option per turn). Without one, a misbehaving coordinator can re-invoke `research` in a loop and burn budget; D-01's stop-criteria belongs both in the sub-agent prompt **and** as an SDK-level cap.
7. **Hooks fire across agents.** `onToolCall` triggers regardless of which agent invoked the tool. Treat this as a verification/observability surface, not a per-agent filter — read the event's agent identity off the event payload.
8. **The streaming chunk format from the SDK is not the AI SDK 6 `UIMessageChunk` shape directly.** A small adapter inside the Hono SSE handler maps SDK events to the assistant-ui transport. Don't pipe raw SDK events to the browser.
9. **`settingSources: ['./CLAUDE.md']` reads at process start, not per turn.** Editing CLAUDE.md does not hot-reload; restart `bsp serve` after coordinator-prompt changes. (For dev, `tsx watch` handles this.)

## 4. Implementation Guidance

**Model Configuration:**

| Agent | Model | Reason | Notes |
|-------|-------|--------|-------|
| Coordinator | `claude-opus-4-7` | Reasoning + pushback substantiveness (CRIT-01) is the load-bearing skill. Opus's instruction-following on the strict-output / never-quote-sub-agent rules is what makes this work. | One Opus turn per user message. |
| `research` sub-agent | `claude-sonnet-4-6` | Web search → claim extraction is retrieval-shaped; Sonnet is sufficient and cheaper. ARCHITECTURE.md confirms. | Strict Zod output (D-04); SDK retries once on validation failure. |
| `compilation` sub-agent | `claude-sonnet-4-6` | The deterministic Phase 1 renderer does the heavy lifting; Sonnet only does narrative paragraphs / contradiction framing in Phase 3+. Phase 2's compilation invocation is essentially `runCompile()` behind a tool gate. | Per ARCHITECTURE.md row in §"Sub-agent definitions". |

Model parameters: rely on SDK defaults for temperature / max_tokens unless a specific failure mode forces a change. Do not introduce per-call temperature tuning until evals signal a need (avoids parameter sprawl across the three agents).

**Core Pattern:** Hierarchical-coordinator-with-tool-gated-sub-agents.

- One `query()` per chat turn; the coordinator chooses whether to delegate to `research` and/or `compilation` based on the turn's intent.
- Sub-agents return a Zod-validated structured object as their final message; the coordinator never quotes the sub-agent's free-text summary into chat (Pitfall 18 / D-06). Instead, on receiving the structured `claim_ids_written: string[]`, the coordinator calls `onebrain_search` (or equivalent) to re-fetch each row and cite the live `text` / `confidence` / `status` from OneBrain.
- The single-writer-to-vault commitment is enforced **at the SDK tool-allowlist layer** — the coordinator's `allowedTools` does not include `mcp__vault__vault_write_atomic`; only `compilation`'s `tools` does. Any other agent attempting the call receives a tool-not-found from the SDK before it reaches the implementation. This is the protocol-layer guard; `src/compilation/vault-writer.ts` is also called only from the compilation sub-agent's tool wrapper, providing belt-and-braces.
- The `repo.writeClaim()` quantitative-claim filter (AGENT-08 / Pitfall 19) is the schema-layer guard: any TAM-shaped or ≥$1M numeric claim arriving without a `cites_source` edge to a real source row is rejected at insertion. The agent-tool wrapper for `onebrain_write_claim` enforces source-row-first ordering (D-05) at the protocol layer. Two layers — see Section 1's failure mode #2.
- The Hono `POST /chat` SSE handler iterates the `query()` async iterator, maps each SDK event to a Vercel AI SDK 6 `UIMessageChunk`, and forwards the SSE stream to `AssistantChatTransport` in the browser. Tool-call events go to two sinks simultaneously: (a) the SSE stream for assistant-ui's tool trace (UI-03), and (b) optional Phoenix tracing (Section 7) when `PHOENIX_ENABLED=1`.

**Tool Use:**

- **OneBrain tools** (`src/agents/tools/onebrain.ts`): `onebrain_write_source`, `onebrain_write_claim`, `onebrain_write_edge`, `onebrain_search`. Thin wrappers over `src/onebrain/repo.ts` — the wrappers add: (1) Zod input validation per `tool()` discipline, (2) source-row-first enforcement on `onebrain_write_claim` (D-05), (3) tag canonicalization via `canonicalizeTag()` on incoming `topic_tags[]` / `framework_tags[]` (D-02 + DATA-10).
- **Tavily tools** (`src/agents/tools/tavily.ts`): `tavily_search`, `tavily_extract`, `tavily_crawl`. Default Phase 2 behavior is search + extract on the top 3–5 hits (D-03); `tavily_crawl` is wired as a capability but not invoked by default — reserved for an explicit user "deep research" signal that lands in Phase 4.
- **Vault tools** (`src/agents/tools/vault.ts`): `vault_read` (any agent that needs it can read; useful for the compilation sub-agent to detect human-edit drift in Phase 3); `vault_write_atomic` (compilation sub-agent only — single-writer enforcement). The implementation calls `runCompile()` from `src/compilation/runner.ts` and `writeAtomic()` from `src/compilation/vault-writer.ts` — Phase 1 code, unchanged.
- **Tool-call narration (D-08).** The coordinator emits a one-line chat message ("Researching Acme's pricing model on the web…") immediately before invoking a sub-agent so the user has prose-level awareness of the in-flight call; the SDK's `onToolCall` hook simultaneously streams the structured tool event to the UI's tool trace. Two channels, neither suppressed.

**State Management:**

- **Conversation state lives only in the SDK's per-turn message list.** Phase 2 does NOT persist chat threads to Postgres — each turn is a fresh `query()` invocation; the user starts a new chat each session. This is intentional (deferred per CONTEXT.md "Deferred Ideas").
- **Durable state lives in OneBrain.** Sub-agents' work product (sources / claims / edges) lands in Postgres before any chat reply is finalized. The wiki view is a derivative of OneBrain — the user can lose the chat thread and lose nothing of substance.
- **`event_log` rows** (Phase 1 schema) capture the audit trail of writes — reads by the coordinator's "dirty count" status pill (D-16: `claims WHERE updated_at > MAX(compile_runs.finished_at)`).
- **The vault** is a write-only-by-compilation derivative; treat it as a cache. The "Last compiled at" status pill (D-16) is computed off `compile_runs.finished_at`.

**Context Window Strategy:**

- **Coordinator turn budget.** A normal Phase 2 turn fits comfortably inside Opus's window: system prompt (CLAUDE.md, ~3-5k tokens) + recent chat (a session, not a thread; ~5-15k tokens typical) + sub-agent structured returns + retrieved OneBrain rows (top-K hybrid search, ~10-30 rows, ~3-5k tokens). Aggregate per-turn coordinator context: ~15-30k tokens — well within Opus's window. Phase 2 does not require automatic context compaction.
- **Research sub-agent context isolation.** The Agent SDK keeps each sub-agent's context separate from the coordinator's by design — only the sub-agent's final structured message returns to the coordinator. This is the architectural lever that lets the research sub-agent extract from 20 web pages without poisoning the coordinator's reasoning. **Don't undo this** by piping sub-agent transcripts back into the coordinator for "transparency" — the tool-trace channel (UI-03) is the user-facing transparency surface.
- **Compilation sub-agent context.** Phase 2's compilation call passes only the affected `topic_tags` (or `all` for the unconditional Phase 2 recompile) plus the OneBrain rows for those topics — Phase 1's renderer is deterministic and does most of the work, so the sub-agent's prompt + retrieved rows fit easily inside Sonnet.
- **Hard caps the SDK does not enforce for you.** Set: (a) `maxSubAgents` per turn (sane default: 3 — Phase 2 has at most one research + one compilation per turn); (b) D-01's research sub-agent stop criteria (~10 claims OR ~120s) inside the prompt + a `tool()`-layer counter that returns elapsed-time/claim-count on every call.

## 4b. AI Systems Best Practices

> Cross-cutting patterns every developer building AI systems needs — independent of framework choice. The runtime here is TypeScript, so where the GSD template references Pydantic, the equivalent here is Zod (per Phase 1 D-21: single-source-of-truth Zod schemas in `src/onebrain/types.ts`).

### Structured Outputs with Zod

The research sub-agent's final message is parsed against a Zod schema; the SDK retries once on parse failure, then surfaces a typed error the coordinator catches and renders into chat. This is D-04 made concrete.

```ts
// src/onebrain/types.ts (alongside existing Phase 1 schemas)
import { z } from 'zod';

export const ContradictionRefSchema = z.object({
  existing_claim_id: z.string(),                    // ULID of the claim already in OneBrain
  new_claim_id: z.string(),                          // ULID of the claim this turn just wrote
  reason: z.string().max(280),                       // one-sentence explanation
});

export const ResearchOutputSchema = z.object({
  summary: z.string().max(900, 'summary must be ≤ 150 words'),  // for coordinator reasoning ONLY — never quoted to chat (D-06)
  claim_ids_written: z.array(z.string()).max(10),               // ULIDs the coordinator re-fetches before citing
  notable_contradictions: z.array(ContradictionRefSchema).max(5),
  proposed_tags: z.object({
    topic: z.array(z.string()),                                  // canonicalized by coordinator (D-02)
    framework: z.array(z.string()),
  }),
});
export type ResearchOutput = z.infer<typeof ResearchOutputSchema>;
```

```ts
// src/agents/definitions/research.ts — wiring the schema into the sub-agent
import { ResearchOutputSchema } from '@/onebrain/types.js';

export const research = {
  description: 'Web search + claim extraction. Stops at ~10 claims or ~120s.',
  prompt: researchPrompt,                  // tells the model to return ResearchOutputSchema-shaped JSON
  model: 'claude-sonnet-4-6',
  tools: [/* tavily_*, onebrain_write_*, onebrain_search */],
  outputSchema: ResearchOutputSchema,      // SDK retries once on parse failure
  // On second failure, SDK surfaces a structured error; coordinator renders it as a system message.
};
```

Coordinator-side handling:

```ts
const sub = await invokeSubAgent('research', { prompt: userQuery });
// sub.output is ResearchOutput (parsed) — never use sub.text or sub.summary directly in chat (Pitfall 18).
const claims = await Promise.all(
  sub.output.claim_ids_written.map((id) => repo.findClaim(id))
);
emitChat(buildReplyFromLiveClaimRows(claims));   // citation strings carry text + confidence + status from OneBrain, not the sub-agent's prose.
```

### Async-First Design

- Every Agent SDK entry point (`query()`, sub-agent invocation, tool implementations) is async. The Hono `POST /chat` handler is itself an `async (c) => { ... }`; do **not** wrap an async iterator in `Promise.all` — iterate it with `for await`.
- **The one common mistake:** awaiting a tool's full result inside a hook callback. Hooks are fire-and-forget — emit-and-return. Doing real work inside `onToolCall` blocks the SDK's event loop and stalls the SSE stream. The trace sink should be a non-blocking `EventEmitter` or buffered queue.
- **Stream vs. await.** Stream: the coordinator's text reply, tool-call events, sub-agent intermediate progress (when the SDK exposes it). Await: the sub-agent's final structured output (Zod-validated; needed atomically before the coordinator's reply finalizes), `onebrain_*` writes (transactional — Phase 1 contract), `vault_write_atomic` (atomic-rename semantics — `src/compilation/vault-writer.ts`).
- The SSE handler is the boundary: SDK events stream through, but each batch of `onebrain_write_*` calls inside a single sub-agent turn is awaited transactionally before the sub-agent's final message lands.

### Prompt Engineering Discipline

- **Coordinator prompt = `CLAUDE.md`, loaded via `settingSources`.** Don't fork the file — there is one CLAUDE.md and it is the live identity-and-protocol spec. Phase 2 plan/execute writes the v2 of CLAUDE.md (the Phase 1 version is generic; Phase 2 adds the write protocol, sub-agent usage rules, pushback templates D-07, hypothesis framing D-09, and the never-quote-sub-agent clause D-06).
- **Sub-agent prompts are static system prompts in `src/agents/definitions/<agent>.ts`.** Each prompt opens with: role (one sentence), the Zod-schema-shaped output contract spelled out as a JSON example, the tool palette as a numbered list, hard stop criteria (D-01 for research), and forbidden behaviors (research must not invoke `vault_*`; compilation must not invoke `tavily_*`).
- **Few-shot examples land in the sub-agent prompts**, not the coordinator's. Two examples per sub-agent: one happy-path (3-claim research turn with one existing-claim contradiction surfaced); one error-path (Tavily 5xx → structured error in summary; zero claims written).
- **Token budget per agent.** Coordinator system prompt ≤ 6k tokens (CLAUDE.md current ~2k; Phase 2 additions stay under 4k). Sub-agent prompts ≤ 3k tokens each. If a sub-agent prompt creeps past 4k, that's a signal a sub-sub-agent split is needed — note it for Phase 4.

### Context Window Management

(Detailed strategy in Section 4 → "Context Window Strategy.") The agent-systems pattern here:

- Keep sub-agent contexts isolated by design — let the SDK do its job. Don't pipe sub-agent transcripts into the coordinator.
- Use Zod-shaped sub-agent returns as the compaction surface — a `ResearchOutput` is ~200 tokens vs. the sub-agent's potentially 50k-token internal context.
- Hybrid search (DATA-09) with top-K limits is the compaction pattern for OneBrain → coordinator: small K (≤30 rows) prevents the coordinator's context from ballooning when claim counts grow.
- Phase 2 does not require SDK-level automatic compaction. Phase 4+ may, once the coordinator regularly exceeds 30k tokens of retrieved context — defer until measured.

## 6. Guardrails

> **Phase 2 framing.** This is a single-user, local-only system with no production deployment, no external user surface, and no data egress beyond Tavily/Anthropic API calls. The "Block / Escalate / Flag" classification from the eval reference is **mostly N/A** in its strict form — there is no end-user to block from and no on-call to escalate to. The functional equivalents in this system are (a) tool-permission gates that prevent forbidden agent capabilities at the SDK layer, and (b) schema-level coercive boundaries at `repo.writeClaim()` that no agent can bypass. These are guardrails in the sense that matters: they are *online, real-time, every-request* checks that fail closed.

### Online (Real-Time) — Tool-Permission Gates and Schema Coercion

| # | Guardrail | Trigger | Intervention | Implementation Site |
|---|-----------|---------|--------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Single-writer-to-vault tool gate** | Any agent other than `compilation` attempts to invoke `vault_write_atomic` | **Reject at SDK tool-permission layer** — agent receives a structured tool-call error; vault filesystem untouched. Logged via pino + Phoenix span attribute `guardrail.violation=true`. | Claude Agent SDK per-agent tool allowlist (`agents/definitions/research.ts`, `agents/definitions/coordinator.ts` — neither lists `vault_write_atomic`). Plus a defensive runtime check in `tools/vault.ts` that asserts `agent_id === 'compilation'` as belt-and-braces. |
| 2 | **Quantitative-claim hallucination filter (schema layer)** | Any caller (agent or otherwise) attempts to write a claim whose `text` matches the TAM-shaped / ≥$1M numeric pattern with empty `cites_source_ids[]` | **Reject at `repo.writeClaim()`** — throws a typed `QuantitativeClaimRequiresSourceError`; row is not inserted; coordinator surfaces the rejection in chat as a CRIT-01 pushback opportunity (rule + action + path-forward). | `src/onebrain/repo.ts writeClaim()` — coercive boundary added in Phase 2. The filter pattern (regex for `\$[\d.]+\s*(M|B|T)`, "TAM"/"SAM"/"SOM" within 50 chars of a numeric) lives in `src/onebrain/quant-pattern.ts`. |
| 3 | **Quantitative-claim source-row-first protocol gate** | Agent calls `onebrain_write_claim` with `cites_source_ids[]` referencing a ULID not yet present in OneBrain at this turn | **Reject at agent tool wrapper** — wrapper returns a structured tool-call error naming the missing source ULID; sub-agent must call `onebrain_write_source` first. | `src/agents/tools/onebrain.ts` `writeClaim` wrapper — D-05's protocol-layer enforcement, complementary to guardrail #2. |
| 4 | **Sub-agent JSON schema strict-parse** | Research sub-agent returns JSON that fails `SubAgentResearchOutputSchema.parse()` | **Single retry via SDK `outputSchema`**, then on second failure the coordinator emits a structured error to chat naming the failed Zod path; turn ends without continuing on the malformed output. | Claude Agent SDK `outputSchema` config in `agents/definitions/research.ts`. Schema lives alongside Phase 1's Zod conventions in `src/onebrain/types.ts` (D-21 / D-04). |
| 5 | **Sub-agent prose-smuggling guard** | Coordinator's outgoing chat message contains ≥ 12-token contiguous substring overlap with the most recent sub-agent's `summary` field | **Strip / rewrite OR fail-loudly to user.** Phase 2 implementation: `src/agents/coordinator-output-guard.ts` runs after every sub-agent turn before flushing the assistant message; on overlap, the coordinator's reply is replaced with a fallback citing claim rows by ULID, and a pino warn + Phoenix attribute `guardrail.prose_smuggling=true` is logged. | `src/agents/coordinator-output-guard.ts`. The same n-gram helper used in Vitest test #3 lives in `src/lib/ngram-overlap.ts` and is reused at runtime. |

**Latency budget for online guardrails.** Each of the five gates is < 5 ms in the median (regex + set-membership + n-gram overlap on a short string), which is negligible against per-turn Opus latency (seconds). All five run on every relevant agent turn; the discipline is not "guardrails are expensive, be selective" (the eval reference's general rule for user-facing systems) — it is "these five invariants are load-bearing for the project's defensibility-by-construction premise, so they always run."

### Offline (Flywheel) — Sampled Quality Signals

> The flywheel surface is intentionally small in Phase 2. We are not yet running production traffic and we have no scaled volume to sample from. These are the quality signals to *capture during dev use* so that Phase 4's Promptfoo suite has historical material to grade against.

| # | Metric | Sampling Strategy | Action on Degradation |
|---|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Pushback substance score** (dimension #4) | All chat turns where the user asserted a TAM-shaped or ≥$1M numeric claim — captured via Phoenix span attribute `pushback.triggered=true`. Phase 2: user reviews a weekly sample manually using the rubric. Phase 4: replaced by Promptfoo eval EVAL-02. | If < 80% of sampled turns hit all three rubric criteria (rule + action + path-forward), revisit the coordinator's `CLAUDE.md` pushback template; treat it as a CRIT-01 regression. |
| 2 | **Sub-agent retry rate** (dimension #5 signal) | Phoenix span attribute `subagent.retry_count` on every research-sub-agent invocation. | If retry rate > 15% over a rolling 50-turn window, the research sub-agent's prompt or `outputSchema` definition is drifting; tighten the schema example or simplify the output shape. |
| 3 | **Quantitative-claim guard hit rate** (dimension #2 signal) | Counter incremented on every `QuantitativeClaimRequiresSourceError` raise; Phoenix span attribute `guardrail.quant_violation=true`. | If hit rate spikes (e.g., > 5 per dev session), it indicates the user is asserting quant claims faster than the research-sub-agent can source them — a UX signal that the pushback path needs to offer the "want me to research it?" option more prominently, not a system bug. |
| 4 | **Recompile failure rate** (dimension #7 signal) | Phoenix span attribute `compile.error` on every recompile run; cross-check against `compile_runs.error IS NOT NULL`. | Any non-zero rate is a regression — recompile is supposed to be deterministic and idempotent (Phase 1 D-18 hash). Investigate immediately. |
| 5 | **Prose-smuggling guard activation rate** (dimension #3 signal) | Phoenix span attribute `guardrail.prose_smuggling=true`. | Should be near-zero in steady state. A non-zero rate means the coordinator's `CLAUDE.md` "never quote sub-agent prose" clause is being violated and the runtime guard is doing the catching — the prompt needs reinforcement. |

**Sampling cadence in Phase 2:** the user reviews these flywheel signals weekly (or per dev session) via the Phoenix UI at `http://localhost:6006`. No automated alerting in Phase 2 — that's a v2 / OBS-V2 concern.

## 7. Production Monitoring

> **"Production" here means the user's local dev session.** This is a single-user, local-only system; there is no production deployment, no on-call rotation, no PagerDuty, no SLA. "Monitoring" reduces to: the user can see, after-the-fact and during a session, what the agents did and whether any of the load-bearing invariants tripped. The infrastructure for that is Phoenix + the SDK's tool-call hooks + the existing pino logger — nothing more is justified at Phase 2 scope.

**Tracing Tool:** **Arize Phoenix** (self-hosted via Docker on `http://localhost:6006`). OpenTelemetry-backed; instruments Anthropic SDK calls automatically via `@arizeai/openinference-instrumentation-anthropic`; renders multi-agent traces (coordinator → research sub-agent → tool calls) as a hierarchical span tree. Activated by `PHOENIX_ENABLED=1` at boot; opt-in so CI does not require a Phoenix container.

**Key Metrics to Track (Phase 2):**

1. **`guardrail.violation_count`** (Phoenix counter) — sum of activations across all five online guardrails (dimensions #1, #2, #3, #5, #6). Steady-state target: near zero for #1 (single-writer) and #3/#6 (source-ordering); non-zero is expected and healthy for #2 (quant guard) and #5 (prose-smuggling) — those are catching the model's natural drift.
2. **`subagent.duration_seconds` p50 / p95** — research sub-agent turn time. Target: p95 < 120s per D-01's hard time-box; alert (in the loose Phase 2 sense — a Phoenix view) if p95 trends > 90s.
3. **`subagent.claims_written_per_turn`** — distribution. Target: 1–10 per D-01's claim cap. > 10 indicates the cap is being bypassed; 0 indicates the sub-agent is failing silently.
4. **`coordinator.pushback_triggered_count`** — count of turns where dimension #4's CRIT-01 pushback fired. This is a *usage* metric, not a *health* metric — it tells the user how often they assert unsourced claims. Useful for seeing whether the rubric in `CLAUDE.md` is matching real user behavior.
5. **`compile.duration_seconds` and `compile.error_rate`** — recompile turn time and failure rate (dimension #7). Target: error rate = 0; duration p95 < 5s for the Phase-2 single-page compile. Non-zero error rate is a hard regression of Phase 1's hash-determinism (D-18).

**Alert Thresholds (informal — Phase 2):** there is no paging surface. The "alert" in Phase 2 is the user noticing one of the following in the Phoenix UI during dev and acting on it:

| Condition | What it means |
|-----------|---------------|
| `guardrail.violation_count` for the single-writer gate (dim #1) > 0 | **Stop work.** A non-compilation agent successfully wrote to the vault, which violates the project's load-bearing architectural commitment. Treat as a P0 architectural break, not a metric drift. |
| `guardrail.violation_count` for prose-smuggling (dim #3) sustained > 5% of turns | The coordinator's `CLAUDE.md` "never quote sub-agent prose" clause is being defeated by the model. Tighten the prompt; the runtime guard alone is insufficient. |
| `subagent.retry_count` > 0 on > 15% of research turns | The Zod output schema is drifting from what the model naturally emits. Tighten the schema example or simplify the output shape. |
| `compile.error_rate` > 0 | Phase 1's D-18 deterministic-hash invariant is broken. Hard regression. |

**Smart Sampling Strategy (Phase 2):**

For a single-user system with no scaled traffic, "smart sampling" reduces to: the user reviews sessions where one of the five online guardrails fired. Phoenix's UI filter on `guardrail.violation=true` is the practical surface — every flagged turn gets human eyes during dev. This is the Phase 2 equivalent of the eval reference's "weight toward interactions with concerning signals (retries, unusual length, explicit escalations)."

When the user transitions toward production-scale use (v2; not Phase 2), this strategy mechanizes into:
- Sampled batch review of all `pushback.triggered=true` spans (dimension #4 quality signal);
- Auto-flag any turn with `subagent.retry_count > 0` (dimension #5 signal);
- Auto-flag any turn where `coordinator.output_length > 800 words` (a proxy for prose-smuggling — the coordinator should never need that many words because it cites claim rows, not paraphrase).

These are stubs for Phase 4 / v2; Phase 2 ships only the Phoenix instrumentation and span attributes that make the future flagging possible.
